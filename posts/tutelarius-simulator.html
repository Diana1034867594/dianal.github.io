01 · Project Overview

A guardian deity whose survival depends on belief

What would it be like to become a Tutelarius, or 土地奶奶—a small guardian deity whose existence depends on the faith of the local community?

Unlike omnipotent gods whose power comes from distant celestial forces, the Tutelarius in our game was once part of the land itself: perhaps a tree, an animal, or a stone that had existed for thousands of years. Through the beliefs of nearby villagers, she gradually gained consciousness and became their protector.

The player responds to prayers, intervenes in village affairs, and decides how to use limited divine power. Every choice affects the villagers’ faith. If their devotion disappears, the deity gradually fades from existence.

03 · Concept & Scope

Reducing an ambitious idea to a complete demo

We defined the project as a 2D top-down simulation-adventure game with narrative decision-making. Our early ideas included additional regions, mini-games, more complex faith-management systems, and multiple endings.

Because we were working with a small team and a short timeline, we narrowed the scope to one complete gameplay loop: enter a region, hear villagers’ requests, choose whether to intervene, and see how accumulated decisions affect faith, power, and the ending.

This decision helped us deliver a polished multilingual demo instead of a larger unfinished prototype.

05 · Art & Visual Development

A hybrid workflow for a consistent pixel-art world

Coraline led the visual direction and asset production. The game combined AI-assisted atmospheric compositions, pixelation tools, real-world references, manually edited maps and props, and hand-drawn animations created in Procreate.

AI worked best for expressive scenes such as the main menu and game-over screen. It was less reliable for the fixed perspective required by in-game maps, so later village and town assets involved substantial manual creation and post-editing.

The refined workflow was to sketch first, test the scene with placeholders, gather references, create or generate a base asset, then adjust perspective, edges, scale, and color before testing it inside Unity.

06 · Engineering

Building the technical backbone in Unity

Character Movement

I implemented player movement using Unity’s Rigidbody system and coordinated physics, collisions, and the Tutelarius’s floating animation. One memorable bug made the character bounce and spin through the scene instead of floating peacefully.

Camera Control

I used Cinemachine with a Polygon Collider and Cinemachine Confiner to keep the camera within the designed map boundaries.

Scene Transitions

Unity’s SceneManager API handled navigation among the main menu, task selection, village scenes, narrative sequences, and ending screens.

Menus & UI

I implemented the main menu, settings, pause menu, dialogue boxes, choice buttons, location descriptions, and ending interfaces, connecting buttons to C# functions through Unity’s onClick events.

07 · Localization Architecture

Designing multilingual support as part of development

Localization was integrated while the game was being built rather than added after completion. The Chinese source was localized into English and French using Unity’s Localization package.

String Tables

I organized menu commands, settings, dialogue, location descriptions, choices, and endings into multilingual string tables using descriptive contextual keys.

Language Switching

The settings menu connected locale selection to Localize String Event components so TextMeshPro objects refreshed when the player changed language.

Font Support

I created and configured a custom SDF font asset after the default fallback setup failed to render some Chinese glyphs consistently.

08 · Multilingual UI

One interface, three languages

Chinese is often more compact than English or French, so the translated interfaces required adjustments to line breaks, font sizes, padding, button dimensions, and text containers.

09 · Localization Challenges

Solving issues across text, fonts, sprites, and tools

Text Expansion

English and French frequently exceeded dimensions designed around Chinese. We revised wording and adjusted font size, wrapping, alignment, padding, and container dimensions.

Repeated Short Strings

Labels such as “Exit,” “Continue,” and “Pause” appeared in multiple contexts. Clearer contextual keys prevented the wrong strings from being linked to UI objects.

Localized Sprites

The title was image-based. Because Unity placed localized sprites inside the source dimensions, we adjusted typography, spacing, transparent margins, and composition for each language.

XLIFF and TMS Compatibility

Phrase TMS did not interpret the exported source and target metadata as expected, so the short demo was translated and implemented manually. The experience emphasized testing export and round-trip import before scaling a localization workflow.

13 · Lessons Learned

Localization is part of the product, not a final add-on

Build localization into the architecture. Externalizing strings and planning multilingual UI early prevented much more difficult reengineering later.

Naming conventions matter. Clear keys, asset names, and scene structures reduced confusion among development, localization, and QA.

Test the pipeline before scaling. A small export-and-import test would have revealed the XLIFF compatibility issue earlier.

Separate text from visual assets. Editable text was significantly easier to localize than text embedded in textures or sprites.

Localization is cross-disciplinary. Writing, engineering, UI, art, fonts, translation, and testing all influenced the final multilingual experience.

Final Reflection

From a cultural concept to a playable multilingual experience

Building Tutelarius Simulator allowed me to combine narrative design, Unity engineering, localization implementation, and multilingual QA within one project. It showed me how deeply engineering decisions influence localization quality: font configuration, string architecture, UI dimensions, sprite design, and scene organization all affect whether the localized experience feels natural and complete.

Although the game is a short demo, it represents a complete workflow: concept, narrative, asset planning, Unity development, localization implementation, multilingual QA, and final delivery. I am proud that our small team transformed an idea rooted in Chinese folk culture into a playable experience across three languages.
